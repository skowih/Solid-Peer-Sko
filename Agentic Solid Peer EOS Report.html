Section 01

🏢 Company Overview

Electro Optic Systems was founded in 1983 in Canberra by Dr. Ben Greene, who started it as a university spin-out building laser tracking systems for the ANU's Mount Stromlo observatory. For nearly three decades it was a specialist optics shop with a side hustle in remote weapon stations — a niche it fell into almost accidentally when the Australian Army needed a locally-made mount for the Bushmaster protected vehicle in the early 2000s. The 2019 peak at A$9+ was followed by a brutal collapse: a disclosure scandal around unbooked revenue predictions, COVID-19 supply chain breakdowns, an ASIC investigation, and a stock that bottomed below A$0.50. By mid-2022 EOS looked terminal.

Then, in August 2022, the board hired Dr. Andreas Schwer — a 14-year Airbus veteran and former Rheinmetall executive who had most recently restructured Saudi Arabian Military Industries — as CEO. Schwer's first move was to divest the profitable but non-strategic EM Solutions naval-satcom subsidiary to UK-listed Cohort plc for A$144M enterprise value (completed January 2025), repaying all debt and leaving the company with A$107M cash. Simultaneously, the Ukraine war and the drone revolution blew open the counter-UAS market, and EOS's Slinger — a 30 mm airburst autocannon on a stabilised remote mount — walked into an almost empty Western competitive field. The re-rate from A$1.07 (52-week low) to A$11.80 (52-week high) in 12 months is the market digesting this pivot.

Today EOS operates three business lines: Defence Systems (remote weapon stations and counter-drone, the revenue engine), High Energy Laser Weapons (brand-new export revenue anchored by the Dutch and Korean contracts), and Space Systems (commercial space-situational-awareness data sales, structurally smaller but strategically vital). Production is split across Canberra (Australia), Huntsville Alabama (USA), Singapore (HEL facility opened February 2026), and — via the January 2026 MARSS acquisition — Monaco/UK (C2 software and radar integration).

🌍 Industry

EOS sits at a peculiar three-way intersection: remote weapon stations (a slow-moving, ~10% CAGR market dominated by Kongsberg), counter-unmanned-aircraft systems (the fastest-growing defence category on earth at ~25% CAGR), and directed energy weapons (the category the US Army, UK MoD, and Germany are currently pouring billions into but hasn't yet scaled commercially). The drone revolution of 2022–2025 — where a US$500 Mavic quadcopter can destroy a US$3M tank — is the single most important economic fact in modern land warfare, and the cost-curve crisis it created (firing US$2M Sea Sparrows at US$2K Shahed drones over the Red Sea) is what's driving every Western ministry of defence to buy cheaper kinetic and directed-energy answers.

Section 04

🔬 How It Actually Works

The Core Process (plain English)

EOS builds three physically distinct things that all use the same engineering DNA: pointing something with sub-millimetre precision at something small and fast and far away. In the case of Slinger, that "something" is a 30 mm exploding shell and the target is a quadcopter at 2 km. In the case of Apollo, the something is a 100,000-watt beam of invisible infrared light and the target is the same quadcopter. In the case of the Space business, the something is a laser pulse and the target is a 10 cm bolt of debris in orbit 800 km up. The products look different, but the engineering problem is identical: build a platform that can hold a line of sight to ~10 microradians while a truck is moving, the earth is rotating, and the atmosphere is shimmering.

Why It's Hard to Copy

The hard part isn't the gun or the laser — both are commodity items EOS buys from Northrop Grumman or builds with fibre-amp modules. The hard part is the fire control stack: the nested real-time loops that slave a 400 kg turret to a radar track while the Bushmaster it's bolted to drives over Ukrainian mud. EOS has been building this since 2001 and has patents going back that far (US7210392B2). Most new entrants — Anduril, Epirus, Quanergy — are software companies bolting optics onto someone else's mount. EOS is the inverse: a 40-year hardware company that is now bolting on software (via MARSS). The "shoot a drone in a wind tunnel" math is well-understood; the "shoot a drone from a moving IFV in 40°C with dust" physics is 10,000 hours of iterative learning. That's the moat.

💡 The Key Metric That Matters

Pointing stability under mobility: EOS R400 holds <1 mrad RMS line-of-sight error while vehicle moves at 60 km/h over rough terrain (company spec). At 1,000 m range, 1 mrad = 1 m circular error — i.e., still hits the target. Industry median for roof-mounted RWS is ~2–3 mrad. This is the single spec that wins the Kongsberg head-to-head.

🎯 Synthesized Investment Thesis

🧠 The Buffett/Munger question: "Would a quality buyer pay for this at this price?"

Yes — but only conditionally. EOS at A$1.9B market cap against a combat-validated Slinger franchise, a world-first 100 kW HEL platform, a debt-free balance sheet, and a backlog at 2.3× current trailing revenue is not an obvious overpay. A Munger-approved entry is a barbell: small core position now, meaningful add only after the Korean deposit clears.

The thesis rests on three compounding mechanics rarely present in a single small-cap simultaneously. First, EOS is riding the largest defence-procurement wave since the 1980s with a product (Slinger) that is not just "good" but is genuinely the only Western option in its exact niche: lightweight, vehicle-mountable, 30 mm airburst, sub-400 kg, combat-proven. Second, EOS just bought the piece of technology (MARSS NiDAR) that transforms it from a component vendor to a systems integrator — a one-step change in gross margin and a multi-step change in addressable contract type. Third, the short-attack overhang plus the ASIC settlement are clearing simultaneously, creating a narrow but identifiable re-rate window.

The reflexive component (Soros): rising orders → rising stock → rising credibility with defence ministries → more orders. EOS has passed the credibility threshold. It's now in the compounding phase.

🐻 Bear Case #2: Kongsberg Drops Prices, Slinger Margin Compresses

Kongsberg Defence has delivered >23,000 Protector RWS and has sub-scale unit costs EOS cannot match. Kongsberg's 30 mm-integrated CORTEX Typhon is positioned directly against Slinger. Combined with MARSS integration costs, EOS 2027 margin guidance becomes unachievable and the "prime integrator" narrative has no margin to fund itself.

Possible Debunk: Kongsberg has not historically pursued pricing warfare — they are a state-influenced (Norwegian government ~50% owner) contractor that optimises for program stability, not market share. Slinger's combat-proof in Ukraine is a credibility differentiator Kongsberg cannot acquire; Kongsberg Protector with 30 mm is a retrofit, not a native design. EOS has lead time on the Ukraine reference.

🐻 Bear Case #3: MARSS Integration Politics Kill the Prime Thesis

Monaco-based MARSS is a software-first culture; Canberra-based EOS is a hardware-first culture. US$36M up-front + €100M earnout creates a seller-controlled outcome for the first 18 months of integration. History of defence M&A is that software+hardware cultural fusion fails more often than it succeeds (Raytheon/Blue Canyon, L3Harris/Aerojet all endured visible integration drag). If MARSS founders leave or if NiDAR gets bureaucratically starved, EOS paid for a product it can't integrate, and the "prime transformation" thesis falls back to "component vendor with software skin."

Possible Debunk: The earnout structure actually aligns MARSS founders to stay through 2028 and drive sales. EOS's Schwer is German — MARSS is European. The cultural gap is narrower than a typical US-Australia integration. EOS's track record of successful divestments (EM Solutions) and cultural pivots (post-Greene) suggests execution capability.

🐻 Bear Case #4: The Whole C-UAS Market Gets Commoditised Faster than Expected

In 2022, drones were a novelty. In 2026 they're commodity. C-UAS economics are following the same curve. Every major defence OEM is now standing up a C-UAS line. Anduril has a US$20B US Army contract vehicle. Rheinmetall is ramping Skyranger to 400/yr. China will flood export markets with cheaper copies. In five years, Slinger's 30 mm airburst may be a $2M commodity instead of a $5M premium. The re-rate window may be shorter than the pipeline.

Possible Debunk: Defence hardware is one of the few product categories where combat reference genuinely gates procurement. Slinger has the Ukraine reference through 2027 at minimum. The first-mover in proven combat is the default choice for ~5 years in defence — longer than the short-thesis window. And the HEL category has yet to commoditise at all.

🧬 Foundation Science: The Physics You Need to Know

Before you can understand why Slinger beats a Kongsberg retrofit, or why Apollo is genuinely different from DragonFire, you need four physics principles in your head. Every EOS product is a specific engineering expression of these four ideas.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Inertial Stabilisation (keeping a gun still on a moving vehicle)

A gun mounted on a moving truck wants to point in a random direction every microsecond as the suspension jostles and the hull rolls. Stabilisation works by measuring that random motion with an inertial sensor (today, usually a fibre-optic gyroscope with drift rates of 0.01°/hour or better) and commanding electric torque motors to push the gun in the opposite direction, 100+ times per second. The loop is fast enough that the gun barrel, to the target, appears to float in space, independent of what the vehicle does.

The difficulty is closing the outer loop: the gun isn't just stable, it's tracking a moving target, so you need two nested control loops (target-tracking + stabilisation) running together without oscillating. Tuning these control laws is the secret sauce of every serious RWS vendor. Kongsberg has done it for 25 years. EOS has done it for 22. Anduril has done it for about 4.

Governing relationship: Pointing error ≈ (external torque × moment arm) / (loop bandwidth × actuator stiffness). Double the loop bandwidth → halve the error. This is why EOS's stabilisation is better on rough terrain: higher bandwidth control law.

💡 Analogy: Think of it like Apple's optical image stabilisation in your iPhone, but pointing a 30 mm cannon, and the iPhone is strapped to a galloping horse.

Historical note: Fibre-optic gyroscopes were invented at Stanford in 1976; commercialised for defence in the 1990s. The MEMS revolution brought cost down 100× by 2010. EOS's stabilisation platform uses a hybrid FOG+MEMS sensor fusion.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Fibre Laser Amplification + Beam Combining (getting to 100 kW)

A single fibre laser module tops out at roughly 5–15 kW because of thermal damage to the glass at higher power density. To get to 100 kW, you build 10–30 modules and combine their beams coherently (phase-locked — the military approach) or spectrally (slightly different wavelengths multiplexed through a grating — the UK DragonFire approach). Each fibre is doped with ytterbium ions, pumped by a bank of laser diodes, and emits at ~1.07 µm — an invisible near-infrared wavelength chosen because silicon-based drone cameras and most optical windows transmit well at it, and because atmospheric absorption is low.

The output isn't just "a 100 kW beam." It's a 100 kW beam with diffraction-limited quality (M² < 1.3 is the production target), tight enough that at 1 km it forms a spot perhaps 5–15 cm across — small enough to burn through a drone's battery pack in 1–3 seconds. That's the lethality budget: ~3 kJ/cm² deposited in target for reliable kill.

Governing relationship: Target irradiance = laser power × aperture² / (range² × beam quality). Double the range → 4× power requirement, all else equal.

💡 Analogy: A single fibre laser is a garden hose. Beam combining is a hundred garden hoses fused into one fire hose — except you've also arranged all the water molecules to march in step so the stream doesn't spread out.

Historical note: Fibre lasers were developed at Southampton in the 1990s. nLIGHT delivered the first 300 kW combined fibre laser in 2023. Apollo is the first 100 kW-class weaponised export.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Adaptive Optics (correcting for atmospheric turbulence)

Earth's atmosphere is a lumpy, turbulent lens. Over a 10 km horizontal path, turbulence spreads a 1 cm beam into a 10 cm smear. To fight back, you measure the wavefront as it arrives (with a Shack-Hartmann sensor that dices it through a grid of tiny lenses onto a CCD — the pattern reveals the wave's distortion), then command a deformable mirror — dozens to thousands of actuators pushing and pulling on a reflective membrane at kHz rates — to pre-distort the beam in exactly the opposite way. What leaves the gun arrives at the target flat.

EOS uses adaptive optics twice: to improve the outbound laser beam in Apollo, and to improve inbound photons in Space Systems (tracking a 10 cm bolt of orbital debris against a daytime sky requires seeing-limited to diffraction-limited imaging, a 30–50× resolution improvement).

Governing relationship: Atmospheric Fried parameter r₀ (typical: 5–20 cm) is the patch of sky over which turbulence is correlated. AO system needs actuators spaced at ~r₀ across the aperture. Larger telescope → more actuators needed.

💡 Analogy: Your brain does adaptive optics when you look through heat haze — it averages out the shimmer. A Shack-Hartmann + deformable mirror is doing the same thing, but in 2 ms instead of 200 ms.

Historical note: Invented for military imaging (spy satellites looking up) in the 1950s, declassified in 1991. Now in every major optical telescope. EOS's daytime satellite-ranging capability is a specific application requiring simultaneous solar-background rejection + AO correction — a short list of facilities globally.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Programmable Airburst Ammunition (30 mm that explodes where you want)

Hitting a 30 cm quadcopter at 1 km with a solid shell is a luck game. Hitting it with a shell that explodes 1 m away from it, releasing a cone of tungsten fragments, turns the Pk (probability of kill) from ~5% to ~70%. The way you do it: the fire control computer calculates exactly how long the shell will fly, writes that time-of-flight into the shell's electronic fuze via an inductive coil as the round is chambered (milliseconds before firing), and the shell's internal timer counts down and triggers detonation at the computed point.

This is not esoteric — it's been standard on 25–40 mm autocannons for 15+ years — but it's the entire reason 30 mm exists as a counter-drone option. Without airburst, you need to fire 10–50 rounds to down one drone. With airburst, 1–3 rounds do it. Ammunition cost per kill collapses from ~US$500 to <US$100.

Governing relationship: Kill probability per round ≈ (fragment density at target) × (fragment lethality area) / (target miss distance²). Better fuze timing precision → tighter miss distance → exponentially higher Pk.

💡 Analogy: Think of it as the difference between throwing a baseball at a mosquito versus throwing a tiny grenade near the mosquito. Same baseball, vastly better outcome.

Historical note: ABM (airburst munition) technology matured in the late 2000s. Rheinmetall's AHEAD (35 mm) and Northrop's 30×113 mm MK310 PABM are the two production families. EOS Slinger uses the NG family.

Section T03 · FLAGSHIP PRODUCT

🔬 Slinger — The 30 mm Drone Slayer

What It Is & What It Does

Slinger is a ~376 kg package you bolt to the roof of a JLTV, Bushmaster, MRAP, or IFV. It fuses a 360° electronically-scanned radar, a stabilised day/thermal sight, a Northrop Grumman M230LF 30×113 mm chain gun, and 150 rounds of programmable airburst ammunition into a single operator station that can autonomously slew, track, and fire on small drones from beyond 800 m.

The Science in Action

Slinger is the specific engineering realisation of two principles from Section T01: stabilisation + programmable airburst. The engineering choice EOS made was to build Slinger on top of R400 rather than designing from scratch. That decision gave them 20+ years of mount-engineering debugging on day one — but it also constrained Slinger to R400-class weight, which is why Slinger's vehicle list includes JLTV-sized platforms where Skyranger cannot go.

The second engineering choice was radar: Slinger uses the Echodyne 4D AESA rather than a mechanical scanning radar. AESA has no moving parts, steers the beam electronically, and can simultaneously track dozens of objects — critical because drone swarm tactics can saturate mechanical radars. The tradeoff is cost per radar; EOS accepts it.

💡 Analogy #1: Think of Slinger as an automated point-and-shoot camera for bullets. The radar is the autofocus, the EO sight is the viewfinder, the ballistic computer is the exposure metering, the gun is the shutter. Operator's job is to pick a target and press the button.

💡 Analogy #2: The airburst shell is a smart rain. A solid slug is a single raindrop that has to hit a fly. Airburst is a small cloud of raindrops released right above the fly — much harder to dodge.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Why 30 mm (and not .50-cal)

A .50-cal bullet is 710 grains (46 g). A 30×113 mm airburst shell is ~3,500 grains (227 g) + explosive fill + fragmentation jacket. The .50-cal has to hit the drone directly — low Pk. The 30 mm has to land within ~2 m — 10–30× higher Pk. Ammo cost per round is ~US$10–30 (.50-cal) vs ~US$200–500 (30 mm airburst). But expected rounds-to-kill for a drone: ~40+ on .50-cal vs ~2–4 on 30 mm. Cost-per-kill: .50-cal ~US$600+ vs 30 mm ~US$400–1,200 — competitive on cost and overwhelming on kill probability.

💡 Analogy: .50-cal is a sniper. 30 mm airburst is shotgun slugs with embedded grenades.

Governing relationships specific to Slinger:

  • Detection range vs. drone RCS: 12 km (1 m² RCS) → ~4 km (0.01 m² FPV) → ~1 km (carbon fibre, stealth).
  • Fuze timing precision drives miss distance: ≤1 ms timing → ~1 m miss → Pk ≈ 70%.
  • Magazine (150 rounds) is the hard ceiling against swarms: >50 drones in sequence may exhaust magazine.

🔍 ZOOM IN: The Inductive Fuze Coil

This is the single most underrated piece of engineering in Slinger. As a round feeds into the chamber, a coil surrounding the feed path magnetically induces a short data signal into the shell's fuze — typically encoding a 15-bit time-of-flight value. The round then travels down the barrel at ~2.5 km/s exit velocity. If the fuze is 0.5 ms off, miss distance grows to 3–5 m and kill probability halves. The entire system — coil geometry, induced voltage, shell antenna, internal timer — must survive the 30,000 G acceleration of firing without losing the programmed value. This is non-trivial electromechanical engineering and it's a Northrop Grumman product EOS integrates. It's also a single-source component — see supply chain flag.

🔬 Apollo — The 100 kW Laser

What It Is & What It Does

Apollo is a containerised or vehicle-mountable beam director that projects a coherent 100,000-watt beam of 1.07 µm infrared light from a large gimballed aperture. Within ~3 km, it melts the control surfaces, motor windings, or battery packs of small drones in 1–3 seconds of dwell time. Beyond 3 km and up to 15 km, it saturates optical sensors ("dazzles") — useful against guided munitions using optical seekers. It's the first 100 kW-class Western directed-energy weapon signed on export, sold to a Dutch NATO customer for €71.4M in August 2025.

The Science in Action

Apollo fuses three foundation-science pillars from Section T01: fibre laser combining, adaptive optics, and inertial stabilisation. EOS's specific engineering move was to package all three into a container/vehicle form factor compatible with the same mount family as R800/Titanis — meaning Apollo can swap in for a gun mount on programs already architected for EOS hardware. Beam combining uses coherent phase-locked fibre modules (the military-preferred approach) rather than spectral combining (the UK DragonFire approach). Coherent combining gives slightly better beam quality at some complexity penalty; EOS has not publicly disclosed the exact module count or power-per-module, but reasonable estimates put it at 10–20 modules of 5–10 kW each.

The AO head is the real IP. EOS has been running daytime satellite laser ranging from Mt Stromlo for ~20 years, which required mastering AO in a regime no one else does commercially (correcting for daytime atmospheric turbulence while fighting solar background). That exact expertise transfers into Apollo, where the outbound laser must punch through low-altitude boundary-layer turbulence to hold a 5–15 cm spot on target at 2 km.

💡 Analogy #1: Think of it as a physics-governed welding torch at arm's length, scaled to 2 km. You need both the torch (laser power) and an impossibly steady hand (stabilisation + AO).

💡 Analogy #2: A regular laser pointer loses coherence over 100 m. Apollo keeps coherence over 2,000 m because AO is essentially cancelling the atmospheric lens-distortion in real time — like perfect noise-cancelling headphones, for photons.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Thermal Blooming, the Physics Villain

At 100 kW, the beam heats the air it travels through. Hot air has lower refractive index than cold air — it becomes a negative lens. The beam de-focuses itself. The effect is worse in humid, dusty, or smoky air (more energy absorbed). In dense fog, effective on-target power can drop 90% or more. This is why "100 kW" is a nameplate spec, not a weather-agnostic guarantee.

💡 Analogy: Pouring hot syrup through cold pancake batter — the syrup creates its own channel by warming the batter around it. The laser is the syrup, the atmosphere is the batter.

Governing relationships specific to Apollo:

  • On-target irradiance ∝ Power × Aperture² / (Range² × M² × atmospheric transmission)
  • Dwell time to kill ∝ Target mass × Heat capacity × Melting temperature / (Irradiance × absorption coefficient)
  • Real-world effective range in clear air: ~3 km (hard-kill Class-1 UAS); in fog/dust, may drop to <1 km

🔍 ZOOM IN: The Adaptive Optics Head

Apollo's AO head is where EOS's 20-year Mt Stromlo heritage directly transfers to weapons applications. Incoming return light from the target is sampled by a Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor (a grid of ~100–400 lenslets imaging onto a high-speed CCD running at >1 kHz). The CCD sees a pattern of spots, each offset from its nominal centre in proportion to the local wavefront slope. A reconstructor (FPGA or GPU) computes the aberration map in ~200 µs and commands a MEMS or magnetic-actuator deformable mirror (dozens to hundreds of actuators) to push/pull the beam surface into an inverse shape. The outbound Apollo laser then reflects off that pre-distorted mirror, travels through the atmosphere's actual distortion, and arrives at the target as a near-flat wavefront. The whole loop runs at ~1 kHz, approximately 10× faster than atmospheric turbulence evolves. This is the single hardest engineering element of Apollo. Fewer than 10 companies globally have production-grade AO heads at this spec.

🔬 EOS Space — Laser-Ranging the Final Frontier

What It Is & What It Does

EOS Space operates laser-based satellite and debris tracking from Mt Stromlo (ACT), Learmonth (WA), and partner sites globally. It fires a pulsed laser at an orbiting object, measures the time-of-flight of returning photons, and computes orbits with accuracy <1.5 m on 10 cm targets at 800 km altitude. Customers: US Space Force / USSPACECOM, ADF, commercial satellite operators.

The Science in Action

Daytime tracking requires rejecting sky solar background at the same wavelength as signal photons — via narrow spectral filtering (<0.1 nm), spatial filtering via AO, and gating single-photon detectors to the expected return window. EOS is one of a handful of facilities globally with demonstrated daytime SLR.

💡 Analogy #1: Seeing a firefly against a searchlight. AO + filtering + timing make the firefly visible.

💡 Analogy #2: Echolocation with photons. Speed of light is known; timestamps give cm-precision distance.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Single-Photon Timing

Fire a ns-duration laser pulse. Photons travel ~1.6 million metres round-trip to 800 km altitude. Only a few return — sometimes one. A single-photon avalanche diode (SPAD) detects that photon and timestamps it to ~10 ps RMS precision. 10 ps = 1.5 mm of ranging.

Environmental Constraints

  • Weather: HEL effective range degrades steeply in fog/rain/dust.
  • Temperature: −40 to +60°C typical; edge performance degrades.
  • Vibration: Re-boresight necessary under extreme mobility.
  • EW contested: Sensor feeds can be disrupted.

Failure Modes & Mitigations

  • Pump diode aging — modular replacement.
  • Gimbal bearing wear — field-replaceable with inspection intervals.
  • AO sensor saturation — gating + filtering.
  • Software race conditions — real-time OS + cert rigour.

🎯 The Hard Truth

Lasers are not magic. 100 kW is a clear-weather, cold-target, stationary-platform spec. In real deployment — rain, dust, Red Sea humidity, moving hardened targets — effective power is half or less. This is why every competent C-UAS doctrine pairs HEL with 30 mm airburst and RF jamming. The single most important fact about EOS: Apollo inside Titanis is the answer, not Apollo alone. Market values Apollo standalone; operational thesis requires layered deployment.

🤝 Customer Relationships & Channel Dynamics

Stickiness Assessment

Technical lock-in: RWS integration into vehicle program (wiring, CAN-bus, displays, spares training) = 12–24 month painful swap. Most programs don't swap for 10+ years.

Data lock-in: Post-MARSS, NiDAR operational data creates classifier improvement over time. Switching loses data value.

Certification lock-in: Each cert is vendor-specific. Program re-cert to different vendor is multi-quarter.

🟢 Stickiness: HIGH

Defence platforms stay in-program 10–20 years. R-series tenures average ~10 years. Slinger follows same pattern. Apollo will be multi-decade. MARSS's 60+ sites are a moated installed base.

Why They Get Chosen

Three consistent justifications in contract announcements. First, Australian Army selecting R400 for Redback IFV (2025, Army Recognition) — sovereignty and SWaP. Second, Ukraine buying R400s (2023–24, euromaidanpress) — combat reliability under extreme field conditions. Third, Dutch MoD HEL selection (Aug 2025, defence-industry.eu) — no alternative Western vendor could guarantee a 100 kW production-ready weapon on 2026–27 delivery.

Common thread: EOS wins when the customer has a specific technical requirement the bulk-volume competitor can't meet. EOS loses pricing wars to Kongsberg on vanilla RWS.

Channel & Distribution

Hybrid: direct-to-government for large programs + platform-integrator partnerships (General Dynamics Land Systems US$22M Dec 2025; Northrop Grumman for Ukraine; Calidus UAE JV). Hybrid structure captures component margin from primes and full-stack margin from direct. Post-MARSS, direct-channel mix should increase.

Repeat Purchase & Expansion

UAE: 2018 A$450M → R200 → R500 JV 2025. Ukraine: initial Slinger → 160-unit follow-on (Sept 2023). US: Slinger trials → Huntsville localisation (2024) → GDLS US$22M (Dec 2025). Each: customers who evaluate EOS and deploy then expand.
