Section 01

🏢 Company Overview

Avio S.p.A. is Italy's monopoly rocket builder and Europe's second-most important space launch company. Headquartered in Colleferro, south of Rome, Avio traces its lineage to 1908 when Pomilio — a pioneer of Italian aviation — first built engines in the same factory complex where Vega rockets are assembled today. The modern Avio was forged in 2003 from Fiat's aerospace spinoff, passed through Cinven's private equity hands, sold its aero-engine division to GE Aviation for €3.3 billion in 2013, and re-listed on Milan's STAR segment in 2017 via a SPAC merger with Space2.

The company does three things: it builds and launches the Vega family of rockets for the European Space Agency (ESA), it manufactures solid rocket boosters (the P120C/P160C) for ArianeGroup's Ariane 6 heavy-lifter, and — increasingly — it makes solid rocket motors for tactical missiles in the defense sector. That third business is the pivot point. Defense has grown from ~5-6% of group revenue historically to ~20% in 2025, and management's ten-year plan targets defense at 60% of sales by 2035.

In late 2025, Avio completed a transformational €400 million capital increase and broke ground on a $500 million solid rocket motor factory in Hurt, Virginia. Raytheon and Lockheed Martin have both signed preferred access agreements for the facility's output. This is no longer just a European institutional space company — it's becoming a transatlantic defense propulsion supplier operating at the intersection of two structural mega-trends: European rearmament and the US solid rocket motor supply shortage.

Section 02

🌍 Industry

Space Launch Market

The global space launch services market was valued at roughly $21 billion in 2025 and is projected to grow at a 12-15% CAGR through 2035, reaching $57-71 billion. The drivers are mega-constellations (Starlink, Kuiper, Guowang), Earth observation demand, and defense/government satellite programs. Europe specifically needs 5-6 Vega C launches per year and 6-9 Ariane 6 launches — all requiring Avio's P120C boosters.

Defense Propulsion

The US solid rocket motor supply chain is critically constrained. Only two incumbents exist — Aerojet Rocketdyne (L3Harris) and Northrop Grumman (ex-ATK). The DoD has explicitly identified SRM production bottlenecks as a national security risk. The ReArm Europe plan envisions up to €800 billion in additional defense spending through 2030. Missile replenishment after Ukraine drawdowns and air defense expansion create unprecedented demand for solid propellant systems.

The structural dynamics are unusually favorable: demand is surging on both sides of the Atlantic while supply is constrained by decades of consolidation and underinvestment. Avio's entry as a third SRM supplier in the US fills a genuine industrial gap that the Pentagon wants filled. In Europe, the company is a monopoly provider of the P120C/P160C boosters that power both Vega C and Ariane 6 — you cannot launch a European rocket without Avio content.

Section 04

🔬 How It Actually Works

The Core Process

Avio makes two fundamental things: solid rocket motors and complete launch vehicles. A solid rocket motor is essentially a tube — a massive carbon-fibre composite casing — filled with a carefully mixed, cast, and cured propellant grain (think: a giant candle, but instead of wax it's a mixture of aluminium powder, ammonium perchlorate, and a rubber binder). When ignited, the propellant burns from the inside out in a precisely engineered geometric pattern, producing enormous thrust. The P120C motor burns 143.6 tonnes of propellant in about 2 minutes, generating an average 4,500 kilonewtons of thrust — equivalent to 15 modern airliner engines running simultaneously.

The launch vehicle side (Vega C) stacks four stages on top of each other like Russian nesting dolls of decreasing fury. The P120C first stage gets you off the ground. The Zefiro 40 second stage takes over at altitude. The Zefiro 9 third stage refines the orbit. The AVUM+ liquid upper stage does the final precision work — coasting, restarting, and precisely releasing satellites. Avio designs, manufactures, integrates, and since December 2025, directly commercializes the entire system.

Why It's Hard to Copy

Solid rocket motors sound simple — tube plus fuel — but the manufacturing tolerances are brutal. The propellant grain geometry determines the thrust curve: get it wrong by millimeters and your rocket either underperforms or explodes. The carbon-fibre casing must be wound in a single monolithic piece (no joins or welds allowed). The nozzle throat operates at temperatures exceeding 3,000°C. And qualification takes years — you cannot simply build a solid rocket motor factory and start shipping product. Each new motor design requires multiple full-scale static fire tests, each costing millions. Avio has over 50 years of institutional knowledge in this craft. The US DoD recognized this by selecting Avio specifically because no new domestic SRM entrants have successfully scaled in decades despite massive demand.

The Key Metric That Matters: Propellant Throughput

2,000 tonnes/year target capacity (Virginia plant at full scale)

Here's why that number is enormous: a typical tactical missile SRM uses roughly 50-150 kg of propellant. At 2,000 tonnes/year, the Virginia plant could produce propellant for 13,000 to 40,000 tactical rocket motors annually — enough to meaningfully move the needle on the US missile production backlog. For context, the initial capacity target is 700 tonnes/year, scaling up over time. At defense-grade SRM pricing (typically $150K-$500K+ per motor depending on size and qualification), even 700 tonnes of throughput translates to hundreds of millions in annual revenue at margins far above Avio's current 6% EBITDA. The US currently has only two SRM producers, and both are capacity-constrained — the DoD has identified this as a critical bottleneck and invested nearly $1B in FY2025 alone to expand the supply base. Avio's Virginia plant is the first new entrant in decades.

🐻 Proposed Ironclad Bear Cases

🐻 Bear Case #1: The Virginia Plant Becomes a Money Pit

Building a greenfield SRM factory is one of the hardest industrial construction projects imaginable. Propellant mixing and casting facilities require specific environmental permits, explosion-proof infrastructure, and ITAR-compliant security. A company with €30M EBITDA is betting $500M on successful execution in a foreign country. If construction delays push operational start to 2029+ and costs escalate by 30-50%, Avio could find its €600M cash cushion rapidly eroding while defense revenue fails to materialize on schedule. The flat EBITDA guidance through 2028 means the company has zero earnings buffer during the build phase.

Possible Debunk: Avio hired VADM Jim Syring — former head of the US Missile Defense Agency — to run Avio USA. The facility design draws on Avio's existing Colleferro plant experience. The capital raise was fully subscribed and the project enters construction debt-free, though there is limited excess cash beyond the planned spend. And Raytheon's MoU includes support for the facility establishment, suggesting the customer is invested in the project's success. But construction risk is real and shouldn't be dismissed.

🐻 Bear Case #2: SpaceX Makes Vega Commercially Irrelevant

SpaceX's Falcon 9 rideshare program offers launch to LEO for as little as $1M per slot vs. ~€35-40M for a dedicated Vega C mission. If ESA's institutional customers begin shifting commercial payloads to SpaceX (as some already do for schedule certainty), Vega C's commercial pipeline could shrink to ESA-only institutional missions. The broader risk: Europe's political commitment to sovereign launch access could weaken if SpaceX becomes "too cheap to refuse." Vega E development (€350M+ invested) could become stranded capital if the market doesn't support the launch cadence needed to amortize it.

Possible Debunk: European sovereign access is a political imperative, not an economic calculation. Post-Ukraine, EU member states have doubled down on strategic autonomy. ESA has committed to 5+ Vega C launches per year through the late 2020s. And Avio's pivot toward defense means Vega increasingly becomes a smaller share of the overall business. But the margin pressure from SpaceX competition is real and ongoing.

🐻 Bear Case #3: Dilution Has Already Destroyed the Setup

The €400M capital increase expanded shares outstanding from ~26M to ~46M — a 77% dilution. Investors who owned Avio before September 2025 have seen their per-share economics reduced by nearly half. The stock peaked at €65 before the capital raise announcement and now trades at €36. Even if the business plan succeeds, the per-share value creation may not compensate for the dilution. Add the 10% additional delegated authority for future issuance, and further dilution is possible.

Possible Debunk: The dilution was necessary to fund a potentially transformative asset (the Virginia plant). If the plant generates €100M+ EBITDA at scale by 2030-2032, the per-share value creation far exceeds the dilution cost. The capital was raised at market price with 99% shareholder approval — the market endorsed the plan. But the short-term per-share math is unfavorable, and the stock reflects this.

🐻 Bear Case #4: ITAR/Political Risk Could Block US Operations

Avio is an Italian company building missile propulsion in the US. Changes in US-Italy relations, ITAR export control interpretations, or political shifts could restrict Avio USA's operations. The FOCI (Foreign Ownership, Control, or Influence) mitigation structure adds complexity and cost. If a future US administration decides to prioritize domestic-only SRM suppliers, Avio USA's access to programs could be limited.

Possible Debunk: The Pentagon actively solicited foreign SRM capacity. VADM Syring's appointment as CEO of Avio USA signals the company has built serious government relations credibility. The FOCI structure with a US-led board provides compliance cover. But political risk is never fully mitigable.

Soros Reflexivity Loop

Avio exhibits a textbook reflexive dynamic. Rising defense spending expectations → higher stock price → enabled the €400M capital increase → funds the Virginia plant → generates Raytheon/LMT contract wins → validates the defense pivot → attracts more institutional capital → higher stock price. The critical node: if the Virginia plant hits milestones on time, each announcement further validates the story and attracts capital. Conversely, any delay would break the reflexive loop in reverse.

Marathon Capital Cycle Positioning

We're at the beginning of a capital investment cycle in solid rocket motors globally. Decades of consolidation reduced the US to two suppliers; now the DoD is actively adding capacity. Avio's €400M investment positions it ahead of any potential competitor (no other credible entrant exists). In Marathon Asset Management's framework, we're at the "early investment" phase where returns will compound as new capacity comes online and competitors face multi-year qualification barriers to follow.

Section 15

💨 Tailwinds & Opportunities

🌬️ ReArm Europe: €800B Defense Spending Wave

The EU's ReArm Europe plan targets up to €800 billion in additional defense spending through 2030. The SAFE instrument provides €150B in EU-backed loans for defense procurement. Missile systems and air defense are explicit priorities. Germany alone is increasing defense spending from €86B to €108B in 2026. Avio's SRM capability maps directly to missile propulsion demand across MBDA, IRIS-T, and other European systems.

🌬️ US SRM Supply Chain Crisis

The DoD invested $940M in FY2025 alone in munitions supply chain expansion. The US has identified SRM capacity as one of the most urgent bottlenecks. Only two incumbents serve a market with surging demand from Ukraine replenishment, Pacific deterrence stockpiling, and Standard Missile production ramp. Avio enters as the only credible new supplier with existing qualification from European programs.

🌬️ Amazon Kuiper Constellation on Ariane 6

Amazon's Project Kuiper will deploy thousands of LEO broadband satellites, with significant launch capacity contracted on Ariane 6. Each Ariane 6 launch requires 2-4 Avio P120C boosters. The P160C upgrade increases payload capacity to meet Kuiper's tight deployment deadlines. This is a multi-year booster production revenue stream with a creditworthy counterparty.

🌬️ Vega C Direct Commercialization

The ESA's transfer of Vega C marketing from Arianespace directly to Avio (effective 2026) gives Avio ownership of the full launch service value chain. This means higher revenue per launch, direct customer relationships, and the ability to price competitively. Avio has already signed launch agreements with SpaceLaunch (US) and other commercial customers.
