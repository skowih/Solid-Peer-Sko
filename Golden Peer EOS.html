🧬 Foundation Science: The Physics You Need to Know

Everything EOS builds — from drone-killing lasers to telescopes that can track a coin at 36,000 km — rests on a handful of physics principles. Master these, and every product page on their website makes sense.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Stimulated Emission & Fiber Laser Amplification

A laser works by a trick of quantum mechanics called stimulated emission: when a photon passes close to an excited atom, it causes that atom to release an identical photon — same wavelength, same direction, same phase. In a fiber laser, this happens inside a glass fiber thinner than a human hair, doped with rare-earth ions (typically ytterbium). Semiconductor diodes pump energy in, and stimulated emission amplifies it into a tightly focused, single-mode beam.

The genius of fiber lasers is that the fiber itself acts as the waveguide — it constrains the beam into near-perfect quality (physicists call this M² ≈ 1). A single fiber module can produce roughly 1–10 kW of continuous power with 30–35% wall-plug efficiency. To get to the 100 kW+ levels EOS needs, you combine many fiber modules. The key relationship: more fibers combined = more power on target, but beam quality degrades if your combining technique isn't precise.

💡 Analogy: Think of a fiber laser as a garden hose with perfect water pressure. One hose is great; but to fill a swimming pool fast, you need to aim ten hoses at the same spot simultaneously. That's beam combining — and it's harder than it sounds when each hose needs to hit the same square centimetre at 1 km distance.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Beam Combining & Atmospheric Propagation

Getting 100 kW of laser light out of a box is only half the battle. The other half is getting that energy to the target through the real atmosphere. Three effects conspire against you:

Thermal blooming happens because the laser heats the air it passes through, lowering its refractive index, which defocuses the beam — like a lens made of hot air working against you. Atmospheric turbulence (the same effect that makes stars twinkle) randomly bends the beam, smearing the spot on target. And absorption and scattering — particularly from water vapour and aerosols — simply steal photons before they arrive.

The fundamental governing relationship is harsh: power density on target drops with the square of distance. At twice the range, you need roughly four times the power for the same damage. This is why the jump from 50 kW to 150 kW in Apollo isn't just a marketing number — it directly extends effective engagement range against hardened targets.

💡 Analogy: Imagine trying to focus a flashlight beam on a wall across a gym. Now do it through a room full of steam from a shower, while someone jiggles the flashlight. That's what the atmosphere does to a laser beam at 2 km distance. The engineering challenge is maintaining a tight, intense spot despite all of it.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Precision Pointing & Gyro-Stabilised Tracking

Whether you're pointing a cannon at a drone or a laser at a satellite, the physics challenge is the same: keeping an optical axis locked on a moving point in space, from a moving platform, while rejecting every vibration and jolt.

EOS's four-axis stabilisation decouples the sensor/sight from the weapon. In a conventional two-axis system, the sensor and gun are bolted together — every time the gun fires (recoil) or the vehicle hits a bump, your picture jumps. In a four-axis system, axes 1 and 2 point the weapon (coarse azimuth and elevation), while axes 3 and 4 independently stabilise the sensor unit. MEMS gyroscopes detect angular rate disturbances; control algorithms compute correction torques; direct-drive motors execute them — all within milliseconds.

The result is sub-milliradian angular precision, meaning the system can hold a beam or a crosshair steady to less than 0.06° — essential when your target is a drone the size of a pizza box at 1,000 metres, or a satellite 36,000 km away.

💡 Analogy: Imagine trying to keep a laser pointer dot steady on a coin glued to a wall — while you're standing on a moving bus. A two-axis gimbal is like bracing your arm. A four-axis gimbal is like having your eyeball stabilised separately from your hand, each on its own shock absorbers. That's how EOS achieves first-round hits from moving vehicles.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Satellite Laser Ranging — Timing Photons Across Space

Satellite Laser Ranging is beautifully simple in concept: fire a very short laser pulse at a satellite carrying retroreflectors (corner cubes that bounce light straight back to sender), then measure the time between departure and return. Distance equals the speed of light times half the round-trip time.

In practice, it's extraordinarily demanding. The laser fires pulses lasting around 10–200 picoseconds. The beam diverges as it crosses hundreds to thousands of kilometres. Of the roughly 10¹⁷ photons in each pulse, only a handful — sometimes literally single photons — make it back to the detector. The timing electronics must resolve intervals to a few picoseconds (trillionths of a second). Atmospheric refraction introduces path delays that must be modelled and corrected. The result: millimetre-level range measurements to objects moving at kilometres per second.

EOS has been doing this since the 1980s, when they partnered with the US government on the Strategic Defense Initiative. That heritage — building telescopes that can track faint, fast-moving objects and fire precision lasers through the atmosphere — is the foundational technology that later enabled their high-energy laser weapons and space domain awareness services.

💡 Analogy: Imagine throwing a ball at a mirror on a speeding train 500 km away, catching the bounce, and from the round-trip time determining the train's position to within the width of a fingernail. That's what SLR does — with photons instead of balls, and timing accurate to trillionths of a second.

Section 04

🔬 Apollo — High Energy Laser Weapon

A. What It Is & What It Does

Apollo is a truck-mounted, 100 kW-class fiber laser weapon system packaged in a standard 20-foot shipping container. It detects incoming drones via radar, locks onto them with electro-optical sensors, and burns them out of the sky with a focused beam of infrared light — neutralising over 20 Group 1 drones per minute at ranges up to 3 km for hard kill, and disrupting drone sensors out to 15 km. It is the world's first 100 kW-class laser weapon to secure an export contract.

B. The Science in Action

Apollo takes the fiber laser and beam-combining physics from Section 02 and turns them into a field-deployable weapon. The system draws electrical power (from external supply or internal batteries), converts it to laser light through an array of ytterbium-doped fiber laser modules, combines those beams, and directs the resulting 50–150 kW output through a precision telescope/beam director onto a target tracked by the same EOS pointing algorithms that have been refined across four decades of space tracking.

The engineering choices are deliberate. EOS chose fiber lasers over chemical or solid-state bulk lasers because fibers are electrically powered (no toxic chemicals), inherently rugged (no precision-aligned mirrors to shake loose), and modular (add more fibers = more power). They chose to keep the system ITAR-free — no US-origin components requiring export licences — which is why they can sell freely to NATO allies and allied nations without US State Department approval. This is a significant commercial and strategic decision that constrains their component supply chain but enormously expands their addressable market.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Dwell Time & Drone Defeat Mechanisms

A laser doesn't destroy a drone the way a bullet does. Instead, it delivers thermal energy — concentrated heat — to a specific spot on the target's structure. The damage mechanism depends on dwell time (how long the beam stays on one spot) and power density (watts per square centimetre on the surface).

Against a Group 1 drone (typically plastic or carbon fibre, ~2–9 kg), a 100 kW beam focused to a few-centimetre spot delivers roughly 10–100 kW/cm². At this intensity, plastic melts in under a second. The drone's battery may rupture. Control surfaces deform. The flight controller overheats and fails. At longer ranges (beyond 3 km), the beam is less concentrated but still powerful enough to blind or damage optical sensors — disrupting the drone's ability to navigate or relay targeting data.

The key equation is: Dwell time ∝ (target hardness × distance²) / laser power. Doubling laser power halves the required dwell time — which is why scaling from 50 kW to 150 kW is transformative for swarm defence, allowing the system to "move on" to the next target faster.

💡 Analogy: Imagine focusing sunlight through a magnifying glass onto a plastic bottle. It takes a few seconds to burn through. Now imagine a magnifying glass ten thousand times more powerful, focusing the light from the same glass to a much tighter spot. That's Apollo — except the "magnifying glass" is a precision telescope, the "sunlight" is coherent laser light, and the "bottle" is a hostile drone at 1 km.

🔍 ZOOM IN: Beam Director

The beam director is where EOS's four decades of telescope engineering converge with their laser expertise. G. What It Can't Do — Limitations & Physics Boundaries

⚠️ Known Constraints

Weather dependence: Rain, fog, heavy dust, and smoke scatter and absorb laser energy. In heavy rain, effective range drops dramatically. This is a physics limitation — no engineering fix eliminates it, only mitigates it. Kinetic backup is essential.

Hard targets: Against armoured or metallic Group 3 drones (like Bayraktar-class), 100 kW requires longer dwell times and closer ranges. The 150 kW upgrade path directly addresses this.

Thermal management: The system generates ~200 kW of waste heat during continuous operation. In hot climates (desert deployment), cooling becomes a limiting factor for sustained engagement rates.

Power supply: Standalone (battery) operation limits the magazine to ~200 engagements. Extended operations require connection to external electrical supply — meaning generator logistics.

Countermeasures: Reflective coatings, rapid rotation (spreading the beam's heat), and thermal isolation tactics are being developed by adversaries. Higher power and faster dwell times are the counter to countermeasures.

H. Why It's Hard to Build

Building a 100 kW laser is achievable — several companies can do it in a lab. Building one that fits in a shipping container, survives transport on a truck across rough terrain, deploys in under two hours, tracks and destroys 20 drones per minute in field conditions, and does it all without US-controlled components — that's the hard part. The difficulty lies in the integration: managing 200+ kW of waste heat in a confined space, maintaining beam quality through optical paths that vibrate, keeping alignment across temperature swings from -30°C to +50°C, and running sophisticated fire-control software that must sequence targets in real time. EOS's advantage is that their beam-directing optics, tracking algorithms, and gimbal engineering have been maturing since the 1980s SDI program. They didn't start from scratch on Apollo — they applied four decades of space-tracking engineering to a weapons problem.

🔬 Slinger — Kinetic Counter-Drone System

A. What It Is & What It Does

Slinger is a lightweight, vehicle-mounted weapon station that shoots down drones using a 30mm cannon with proximity-fuzed ammunition. Based on EOS's proven R400 remote weapon station, it adds a 4D AESA radar and specialised anti-drone automation to create a self-contained hard-kill system weighing under 400 kg. It's the kinetic complement to Apollo's laser — effective in weather conditions where lasers struggle, and at a fraction of the cost of missile systems.

B. The Science in Action

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Proximity Fuzing — Why You Don't Need a Direct Hit

Hitting a small, manoeuvring drone with a single bullet is extraordinarily difficult. Slinger sidesteps this problem with proximity-fuzed ammunition: each 30mm round contains a tiny radio-frequency transmitter and receiver. As the round approaches a target, it emits RF signals and listens for reflections. When the return signal exceeds a threshold — indicating the round is within lethal radius of a target — it detonates, releasing a cloud of high-velocity fragments that shred the drone.

The fuze must distinguish between a drone and the ground (which also reflects RF). It must arm only after leaving the barrel (safety distance) and self-destruct if it misses (to prevent collateral damage on the ground). The engineering is in the miniature electronics that survive 30,000+ g launch acceleration and function reliably in milliseconds of flight time.

💡 Analogy: Think of it as a tiny fragmentation grenade that detonates when it "senses" it's close enough to a drone — turning a near-miss into a kill. It's the difference between trying to hit a mosquito with a sewing needle (direct hit) versus swatting it with a newspaper (proximity burst).

The stabilisation algorithms — refined through years of live-fire testing, including battlefield feedback from Ukraine where over 200 EOS systems are active — are embedded institutional knowledge, not easily copied from a spec sheet. EOS claims a 30% accuracy advantage over its nearest competitor (per CEO Dr Schwer, DSEI 2025), a figure derived from decades of gimbal and software iteration.

🔬 R-Series Remote Weapon Stations

A. What They Are

The R-Series is EOS's bread-and-butter product line: a family of remotely operated weapon mounts that allow a vehicle crew to aim, track, and fire weapons from under armour protection. The family spans from the ultra-lightweight R150 (12.7mm, designed for logistics vehicles) to the heavyweight R800 (30×173mm cannon, turret-equivalent firepower). Over 2,500 have been delivered worldwide. They share a common software architecture and control interface — meaning a crew trained on one variant can operate any other.

B. The Science in Action

The core technology is the same four-axis gyro-stabilised pointing platform described in Section 02, combined with an integrated ballistic computer. The ballistic computer takes inputs from the weapon type, ammunition data, range (from the laser rangefinder), atmospheric conditions, and vehicle attitude/dynamics — then computes a firing solution in real time. The four-axis gimbal executes that solution while rejecting vehicle vibrations, engine harmonics, and terrain shocks. The result: high first-round hit probability at ranges up to 2 km, from a moving vehicle.

🔬 Space Systems — SDA, SLR & Atlas Space Control

A. What They Are

EOS Space Systems is one of the world's leading providers of ground-based space surveillance. They design, manufacture, and operate observatory systems that track satellites and debris across all orbital regimes — from Low Earth Orbit (300 km) to geostationary (36,000 km) and beyond to cislunar space. Their newest product, Atlas, extends this to active space control: using high-energy lasers to deter, degrade, or disable hostile satellites from the ground.

B. The Science in Action

The space systems apply the same precision optics and laser engineering from the defence side, but pointed upward and with different goals. For Space Domain Awareness, the challenge is detecting faint objects moving at ~7.5 km/s against a background of stars. EOS telescopes (1m and 2.4m aperture) use CCD sensors to image these objects, then compute orbits from the positional data. For SLR, they fire short laser pulses and time their return — the technique described in Section 02. For Atlas space control, they direct scalable laser power at on-orbit targets. EOS describes the effects as ranging from deterrence through to active engagement, with operators able to adjust power levels to match mission needs — though the company does not publicly disclose specific power thresholds for each effect tier.

🔬 THE SCIENCE: Adaptive Optics — Defogging the Atmosphere

To fire a laser through the atmosphere and hit a satellite's sensor package at 36,000 km requires correcting for atmospheric distortion in real time. EOS uses adaptive optics: a deformable mirror whose surface shape is adjusted hundreds of times per second to pre-compensate for atmospheric turbulence. A lower-power guide laser measures the turbulence profile; algorithms compute the required mirror deformation; actuators reshape the mirror surface — all within milliseconds. The result: the high-power beam arrives at the satellite as though the atmosphere weren't there.

💡 Analogy: Imagine you're trying to read a sign through a swimming pool. The water's ripples constantly distort your view. Adaptive optics is like wearing magic glasses that reshape themselves 1,000 times per second to cancel out each ripple, giving you a perfectly clear view — and allowing you to project a laser pointer perfectly onto the sign from the other side of the pool.

Section 08

🔬 Interceptor-MR — AI Drone Interceptor

A. What It Is

Interceptor-MR is an 8 kg, AI-guided drone designed to ram and destroy hostile drones through kinetic impact — no explosives required. Acquired from UK-based MARSS Group in late 2025, it's EOS's entry into the autonomous interceptor category. It launches vertically from a canister, transitions to horizontal flight at up to 288 km/h, and uses an onboard infrared seeker with AI-based guidance to chase down and collide with enemy UAVs at combined closure speeds that can exceed 400 km/h.

B. The Science in Action

The physics of kinetic interception are straightforward: kinetic energy = ½mv². An 8 kg interceptor at 80 m/s carries approximately 25,600 joules of kinetic energy — equivalent to the muzzle energy of a rifle bullet — concentrated into a titanium-reinforced carbon fibre nose. When it collides with a ~5 kg drone at a combined velocity of 100+ m/s, the impact energy shatters both airframes. The interceptor's propellers stop and fold just before impact to survive the collision; if the interceptor remains flyable, it can re-engage or return to base for repair.

Supply Chain Risk: MODERATE

EOS owns its core IP and manufactures critical subsystems in-house, which provides resilience. However, key external dependencies exist: Echodyne (US) for AESA radars, Northrop Grumman (US) for M230LF cannons, and commercial fiber laser diode suppliers concentrated in a limited number of countries. The ITAR-free architecture avoids US export control chokepoints, but does not eliminate geographic supplier concentration. Laser weapons are manufactured at a new Singapore facility, adding geographic diversification but introducing a single-facility risk for HEL production. The biggest vulnerability: commercial fiber laser module supply chains, where a disruption could bottleneck Apollo production.

🔴 The Hard Truth

Lasers are weather-dependent weapons. In clear conditions, they are devastatingly effective and economical. In rain, fog, smoke, or heavy dust — common battlefield conditions — they degrade rapidly. No amount of engineering eliminates this physics constraint. This is why EOS integrates lasers into layered systems (Titanis) alongside kinetic effectors: the laser handles clear-weather volume threats, while Slinger's 30mm cannon provides the all-weather backup. Any assessment of EOS's laser technology must account for this: it is a complement to, not a replacement for, kinetic air defence.

🧩 Swarm Autonomy

As drone swarms become larger and more autonomous, the engagement sequencing problem becomes computationally intense. Section 13

🔮 Technology Trajectory

🐢
Incremental

Power creeps up to 200–300 kW over 5 years. Beam quality improves. Costs decline as production scales. RWS family gets AI-enhanced automation. Atlas matures into operational service. EOS captures growing share of the expanding C-UAS market.

⚡
Step Change

Coherent beam combining breakthrough enables 500 kW+ in current form factor. Effective hard-kill range extends beyond 10 km. Lasers begin replacing missile systems in SHORAD role. EOS's ITAR-free position becomes decisive competitive advantage as NATO demand surges.

🌊
Platform Shift

Autonomous interceptor drones (like Interceptor-MR) mature to the point where they handle the majority of C-UAS engagements. Lasers become one layer in a fully autonomous, AI-orchestrated defence mesh. The integration platform (C2 software, sensor fusion) becomes more valuable than any individual effector.

💀
Disruption

A breakthrough in electronic warfare — such as AI-powered adaptive jamming that can defeat autonomous drones reliably — reduces demand for hard-kill C-UAS. Or: adversary countermeasures (reflective coatings, heat-resistant drones) render current-generation lasers ineffective, requiring a fundamentally different approach.

The most likely trajectory is Incremental trending toward Step Change. Fiber laser power is on a clear scaling curve; multiple government programs (US HELSI, European equivalents) are pushing toward 300 kW–1 MW systems within this decade. The autonomous interceptor layer (Platform Shift) is emerging but is 3–5 years from maturity. The Disruption scenario is the least likely in the near term — the physics of laser-matter interaction is well understood, and adversary countermeasures add cost and weight to drones without eliminating the threat. EOS's position as the first company to secure export orders for ITAR-free 100 kW-class systems gives them a window of advantage that is hard to close quickly.
